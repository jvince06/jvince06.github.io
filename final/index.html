Accidental Drug Overdoses in Connecticut (2012-2024)

Starting in the 1990s, accidental drug overdoses have become a national and worldwide crisis. The United States experiences the most accidental overdoses (according to this Commonwealth Fund report) with 324 deaths per 1 million people. While many states are putting resources into fighting the war on drugs and Fentanyl, the primary reason for overdoses, Connecticut is an example of a state that hasn't seen promising results compared to other states.

The data used for this study was courtesy of Data.gov and is centered around Connecticut from 2012-2024. While examining the data I found a few primary trends that I found interesting, which led me to reach out and interviewed Syracuse University Professor and Coordinator of Addiction Studies, Dessa Bergen-Cico to help me through these trends and tying them to the rest of the country. The first trend I chose to focus on was which were the primary drugs that resulted in accidental overdoses.

To no surprise, Fentanyl was the number one killer when it came to accidental drug overdoses, with Cocaine and Heroin rounding out the top three. Dr. Bergen-Cico has also noticed a rise in overdoses from Cocaine, as she said, "There has been a rise in overdoses with Cocaine, and it is uncertain if it's because the cocaine is mixed with Fentanyl or some other byproduct." It was also interesting to see how many overdoses were caused by the mixing of multiple drugs, with 132 overdoses and many more that were found with multiple drugs in their system after death. It is no understatement to say to avoid mixing narcotics, with the data showing that doing so can be a recipe for an unexpected death.

The map above shows the cases of accidental drug overdoses per county. The leading counties are New Haven and Hartford counties, which makes sense because these are the most populated counties in Connecticut and are also home to major Connecticut urban areas.

The most profound and interesting trend found in the data was the age of which the most overdoses happened. The most common ages for accidental overdoses to happen were 36 (358 deaths) and 54 (356 deaths). I expected the most common ages to be in the 20s and 30s but was very surprised that 54 was the second most common age. When asked about this, Dr. Bergen-Cico said, “I think one of the things that is a little surprising with the people who are in the older age group is that presumably their addiction pathway comes from pharmaceutical use and over prescribing.” This goes to show that overdoses can lead to a large number of deaths even if they are pharmaceuticals.
